ICT in teaching


This page gives an overview of the affordances provided by use of technology in teaching and the influences these have on pedagogy. The contexts for these uses include the use in teaching in the classroom and for teachers' own CPD. The use of technology and its relationship to the standards is discussed and, at the foot of the page, there is a list of useful resources for tutors and trainees.

Information and communication technology (ICT) is more than just another teaching tool. Increasingly the evidence suggests that ICT has the potential to improve the quality and standards of pupils' education. The TDA recognises its considerable potential for supporting teachers, both in their everyday classroom role and in their continuing training and development.

This statement encapsulates the importance of ICT for teachers. This importance is also manifest in the standards for QTS, which include ICT in both professional knowledge and understanding and in professional skills:
  • Q16: Have passed the professional skills tests in numeracy, literacy and information and communications technology (ICT).
  • Q17: Know how to use skills in literacy, numeracy and ICT to support their teaching and wider professional activities.
  • Q23: Design opportunities for learners to develop their literacy, numeracy and ICT skills.
  • Q25 (a): Teach lessons and sequences of lessons across the age and ability range for which they are trained in which they use a range of teaching strategies and resources, including e-learning, taking practical account of diversity and promoting equality and inclusion.
Thus, to be successful, trainees need to develop their own use of ICT and ways to enable their learners to do the same.

Trainees need to learn how to create effective learning and teaching environments, even though resources will differ from school to school. This makes it necessary for tutors to not only talk about ICT in the classroom but also to model best practice by demonstrating different ways in which technology can enhance the delivery of ICT. This applies to all subjects including ICT itself – simply because a trainee is teaching ICT does not mean that their ability to make effective use of technology for learning and teaching can be taken as read. The methods used to deliver ICT through ICT by tutors should enable trainee teachers to integrate ICT within their teaching and provide opportunities for them to use the technologies they will encounter in school.

Tutors should make effective use of this range of technologies both hardware- and software-based. These will include wireless and portable devices, learning platforms, digital multimedia creativity tools and Web 2.0 environments for collaboration, e-portfolios and personal web spaces. Within the context of the school environment, trainee teachers will be required to make effective use of this wide range of resources and preparation for these experiences must be systematically addressed.

Tutors, in order to provide for the need of their trainees, need to be aware of the current thinking about good practice. They should also have knowledge and understanding of relevant research so they can support the needs of the trainees.

The TDA has produced guidance on best practice for ITT providers in the provision of ICT. Again this is aimed at all providers and tutors but its messages are equally pertinent for ICT tutors. On this website, the QTS Standards resource exemplifies the standards in the context of teaching ICT as a subject. It complements the generic guidance on standards produced by the TDA.

An earlier report from Becta summarised the findings from research on ICT and ITT.

Other useful resources

This website's section on research on teaching and learning ICT contains a wealth of further resources.

The following books are recommended as resources for advice for trainees and tutors in ICT:
  • Allen, J, Potter, J, Sharp, J, and Turvey, K (2007) Primary ICT : Knowledge, Understanding and Practice Exeter: Learning Matters
  • Duffty, J (2006) Primary ICT: Extending Knowledge into Practice Exeter: Learning Matters
  • Kennewell, S, (2004) Meeting the Standards in Using ICT for Secondary Teaching London: RoutledgeFalmer
  • Kennewell, S (2006) A Practical Guide to Teaching ICT in the Secondary School London: RoutledgeFalmer
  • Leask, M and Pachler, N (1999) Learning to Teach Using ICT in the Secondary School London: RoutledgeFalmer
  • Miller, J, Higgins, S and Packard, N (2004) Meeting the Standards in Primary ICT: A Guide to the ITT NC London: RoutledgeFalmer

Purposes for using ICT


This page considers some of the purposes for using ICT in the classroom and with trainees.
  • creativity
  • motivation
  • concept development
  • assessment for learning
  • collaborative learning
  • multisensory representation
  • personalisation
  • learning outside the classroom.
These purposes have been chosen for the impact they have on learning and teaching both in schools and with trainees. For each of the purposes listed above, there is a short discussion of the key issues for ICT tutors and resources for further reading.

ICT and creativity

The National Advisory Committee for Creative and Cultural Education (NACCCE) report offers a definition of creativity that has four facets:
  • thinking or behaving imaginatively
  • activity that is purposeful
  • generation of an original artefact
  • outcomes that have value
These four facets are reflected in much that the ICT curriculum offers. Through its focus on purpose and audience, the effective use of ICT provides opportunities for pupils to generate imaginative solutions that yield original products of value to the intended audience. Trainee teachers could reflect on these four when planning lesson objectives and outcomes. Creativity is also fundamental to allowing pupils to explore ideas and to develop authentic learning activities, something that Ken Robinson, former chair of NACCCE, argues schools may not always allow as a matter of course.

To assist with the embedding of creativity in the curriculum, the QCA has a minisite dedicated to creativity. This offers advice on promoting creativity during the planning, teaching and reviewing stages. Further advice came from Loveless in her report on the impact of ICT in creativity for Futurelab and its section on implications for practice has many ideas that are still current. This report was one of the bases for the TDA/Microsoft Futures Forum on ICT and Creativity, part of a three-year UK initiative focused on a programme of activities to help teachers, trainee teachers and the wider school workforce become confident in the use of ICT.

ICT, motivation and engagement

Much research has been carried out into the link between the use of ICT, motivation and engagement of learners.

A report from Becta summarises the key findings and suggests resources for further reading. The report highlights that there is substantial evidence that ICT can have a positive effect on students’ enjoyment and interest in learning. Key benefits identified in the report are:
  • increased commitment to the learning task
  • increased independence and motivation for self-directed study
  • enhanced self-esteem and improved behaviour.
Passey, Rogers et al’s research for the DfES identifies a number of ways in which the use of ICT has motivational effects on learners. These include positive impacts on both in-school and out-of-school learning.

ICT tutors should encourage trainee teachers to reflect on this research to exploit this intrinsic motivational aspect of their subject. Adults and children alike have an intrinsic motivation to progress and the use of ICT offers unique opportunities to learners and teachers to enhance classroom practices and to change attitudes and behaviours. The use of ICT is, of course, not a panacea. ICT when delivered in a dull way does not help to encourage and develop student motivation.

ICT and concept development

Is the most effective use of ICT about doing existing things differently or doing completely different things? Cox and Webb's report on ICT and pedagogy (2003) summarises the findings from research:

The pedagogical practices of teachers using ICT can range from only small enhancements of practices using what are essentially traditional methods, to more fundamental changes in their approach to teaching."

When learners are developing their understanding of new concepts, ICT may provide a new set of tools or approaches. These include, for example, iterative development of ideas through redrafting, use of mind maps, use of multimedia, collaborative authoring, modelling and the use of mobile and e-learning to build opportunities for learning outside of the classroom.

Many of these approaches also make demands for different teaching methods. The Developing Pedagogies for E-learning Resources project provides resources and case studies in this area. The other pages of this Teaching and Learning section also provide ideas in specific phases of education Primary, Key Stage 3, Key Stage 4 and 14-19. Another section of this website summarises the research into the pedagogy of ICT.

ICT and Assessment

Through the use of communication, data handling and presentation tools, ICT provides opportunities for assessment and monitoring of pupil progress. Tutors should explore these with trainees. Examples include
  • Use of spreadsheets or other data handling tools for tracking progress
  • Use of data to analyse value added and expected levels of attainment based on prior achievements
  • Use of VLEs for electronic submission of work and provision of formative feedback through comments and annotation
  • Use of electronic portfolios for collation, presentation and submission of work and for transfer between educational settings
  • Use of e-learning tools for e-assessment and diagnostics
  • Use of e-learning tools for peer review and e-mentoring
Some of these issues are discussed more fully in the assessment section of this website. A further resource on e-assessment is being produced for this website during 2008.

Some key resources include

Black, P. J. & Wiliam, D. (1998). Inside the black box: raising standards through classroom assessment London, UK: King's College London School of Education (see the King's College Assessment website for more information).

Futurelab's e-assessment literature review.

ICT: supporting assessment for learning from the Strategic Leadership and ICT Toolkit

Collaborative learning

ICT provides opportunities for learners to work together and produce a shared outcome. Methods of doing this include
  • Individuals working on sections of a presentation, report or multimedia that are then combined
  • Shared review and redrafting
  • Use of wikis to develop shared content
  • Annotation of peers' work including the use of comments in blogs and discussion forums.
These ideas, as with others on this page, can equally be applied to trainees as to pupils in schools. The use of a wiki for example would allow trainees to jointly plan a lesson and prepare associated resources. ICT allows for collaboration and the web-based wiki allows for this to be done at a distance.

Multisensory representation

ICT allows for ideas to be represented in a range of media. A presentation may be made using text, which is complemented by an audio track. Videos can be subtitled with text. Transcripts of audios can be provided.

ICT provides tools for the creation of multimedia products and some of these allow for the embedding of different media. This 'media redundancy' allows not only for information to be made accessible to pupils with specific needs but also provides choice to all students. It may well be that, in some circumstances, pupils will understand a concept better if it is presented visually. Or they may follow the concepts being presented in a video if they have the transcript available for future perusal.

Personalisation

Personalisation is a key aspect of the Government's Harnessing Technology strategy. ICT supports the personalisation of learning in a number of ways. These include
  • Providing alternative versions of a resource or activity to match need
  • Providing alternative pathways through materials through guided feedback or timed release on a VLE
  • Provision of supporting resources on a VLE for further study outside of the classroom
  • Supporting opportunities for assessment for learning, as above.
Underwood et al's Impact 2007 report for Becta reports on the impact of technology on personalisation. Some of the key findings include:
  • In teachers’ perceptions, it is clear that ICT is strongly associated with personalising learning.
  • Pupils’ perceptions of the level of personalisation of their learning are linked to work ethos including self-efficacy.
  • Primary pupils with a greater opportunity to use ICT and also with more positive attitudes towards computers recorded a more personalised learning experience.
  • For both primary and secondary pupils, the learners’ overall experiences of personalisation were associated with higher levels of pupil work ethos and greater opportunities to use ICT.
  • Allowing content choice is the most frequent way of personalising the learning experience. Learner goal-setting and self-monitoring is far less prevalent.
  • Personalising learning is constrained by the National Curriculum.
  • Computer use and e-maturity are associated with personalising learning.

(Underwood et al:2007:4-5)

More detailed advice is being developed for inclusion in this website for Easter 2009.

Learning outside the classroom

A VLE can make resources, activities and feedback available outside of the classroom. Such uses also allow for the development of home-school links as parents can be given access to some of this information.

ICT can also be utilised in other situations beyond the school, for example, the collection of data on field trips using mobile devices, the use of podcasts downloaded to MP3/MP4 players for mobile learning and the exchange of information between settings such as work placements or where learning takes place in more than one institution.

Resource management and learning platforms


This page considers the development of the use of technology for managing resources. Tutors should enable trainees to develop their understanding of such tools and to have access to their use both in the provider's centre and in placement schools. Topics addressed on this page are:
  • Websites
  • Learning platforms, VLE and MLE
  • Content management
  • Curriculum mapping and planning
  • Learner access and administration
  • Communication tools
Websites

Most schools will have a website and trainees should be encouraged to discuss its use. The main purpose of the website is to market the school to its community, and maybe to act as a portal to other services such as the learning platform, weblogs or print materials such as a prospectus.

As the website is publicly available there are considerations of e-safety. Becta’s guidelines on the safe use of school websites highlights the issues of ensuring privacy and safety in the use of images, weblogs, webcams, etc. There are further issues to be considered if pupils are engaged in maintaining the site.

Learning platforms, VLE and MLE

(See also the Primary section of this website)

The learning platform is the key ICT system to support learning, teaching and associated administration for a school community. It is intrinsically ‘inward-facing’, supporting use by the existing members of the school community. It will be complemented by the 'outward-facing' school website, and the two may be fairly tightly linked through a portal with a single URL.

This page looks at learning platforms, their functionality and impact on the needs of initial teacher training.

Government targets on the access of all pupils to online learning platforms have taken several forms:

“Ensure every learner has appropriate access to technology in school and beyond the normal school day. [by 2010]” (DfES e-strategy)

“By spring 2008 every pupil should have access to a personalised online learning space with the potential to support an e-portfolio and by 2010 every school should have integrated learning and management systems (a comprehensive suite of learning platform technologies)” ("What is a learning platform", Becta).

The provision of such learning spaces is a key aspect of the personalisation agenda (see also the Purposes for using ICT page in this section). Underwood et al's Impact 2007 Report for Becta reports that where VLEs are implemented well they support personalisation and provides case studies of their use in this respect.

The Government defines “learning platform” as

“…an umbrella term that describes a broad range of ICT systems used to deliver and support learning and teaching. It usually combines several functions, such as organising, mapping and delivering curriculum activities, and the facility for learners and teachers to have a dialogue about the activity, all via ICT.”

Further, the definition states that the term learning platform “may be applied to a virtual learning environment (VLE) or to the components of a managed learning environment (MLE). A VLE is a software tool which brings together resources for curriculum mapping, delivery, assessment, tutor support, communication and tracking. A managed learning environment (MLE) refers to the whole range of information systems and processes that support learning and the management of learning within an institution. It includes VLEs or other learning platforms, administrative and other support systems.”

Learning platforms are becoming more widespread in schools as well as in ITT organisations, so trainee teachers will need to understand how they can make use of them to develop and support their pupils’ learning. They should also be aware of the Becta guidance on purchasing systems and the associated functionality checklists. Local authority and school policies on the implementation of learning platforms will be a key issue for many as they move on to become subject leaders. Of particular interest is the debate between proponents of open source and proprietary solutions with their different relative cost models.

There is a vast array of functionality and tools that could be considered by trainee teachers. JISC’s e-learning framework provides a very comprehensive overview of the landscape. It does this from the point of view of higher education and with the emphasis on ‘pure’ e-learning. As an aside, it is interesting that the underlying technology of the JISC e-learning framework site is built on an open source content management system (Plone). Trainees could usefully be made aware of such products and their use in web and system design.

From a schools’ perspective, and with the assumption of classroom interactions to complement the use of the platform, Becta identifies a range functions that would typically be found in a learning platform:
  • Content management
  • Curriculum mapping and planning
  • Learner access and administration
  • Communication tools

Each of these functions will now be considered in turn.

Content management

A learning platform is essentially a collection of resources made available online. From a learning and teaching perspective, many of these resources will be digital artefacts – documents, multimedia, etc. To equate ‘content’ with ‘file’, however, would be to take a very narrow view. Even in the relatively early days of VLEs, Britain and Liber, writing for the Joint Information Systems Committee (JISC), identified that “Virtual Learning Environments (VLEs) … synthesise the functionality of computer-mediated communications … and on-line methods of delivering course materials” (p7). While this report was on the higher education sector, this notion of synthesis of functions is a useful one. The range of functions has increased over time and trainee teachers could be encouraged to share experiences from the diversity of products and implementations they encounter while in schools and colleges.

While there is a multiplicity of functionality available on any given learning platform there will be also be a spectrum of usage of that functionality according to individual teacher or subject. Some will use the learning platform merely as a ‘digital repository’ – storing files created on standard ‘office’ software. Others will use the interactive, collaborative and management tools to more fully embed the platform in the learning of pupils. Trainee teachers need to reflect on these continua and to explore how the particular demands of their subject and the particular context in which they work will allow such functionality to be exploited.

Stephenson (2001) identifies another set of axes when considering the nature of content. Here the originator of the content becomes crucial. For some teachers, and trainees and tutors, the content held on a learning platform is predominately created by themselves. For others, at the other end of Stephenson’s axis, the content is created by the pupils. This echoes back to the nature of creativity in the use of ICT.

Gillespie et al (2007) address the use of learning platforms with and by trainee teachers. They consider the range of ‘content’ that may be deployed and have sharing, collaboration and communication at the heart of this discussion. The use of the learning platform for handling assessments – setting assignments, online submission and feedback are all considered. Feedback here includes formative assessment for learning and summative assessment of learning. Trainees should be encouraged to develop proficiency in using online systems for providing both types of feedback.

Becta’s functionality requirements for learning platforms lists the requirements for ‘approved’ systems. Trainee teachers could evaluate the systems they use against the requirements. These include the ability to handle content for assessment purposes, for interoperability across systems and for accessibility from a range of devices.

Curriculum mapping and planning

A traditional view of curriculum mapping and planning may be that it is something that is done by the teacher and perhaps only fleetingly shared with pupils through handouts at the start, and at key points, of a course. The use of a learning platform provides opportunities to involve the pupils in this mapping and to provide elements of personalisation and differentiation while doing so. It also provides opportunity for plans and resources to be shared across a group of teachers.

The very nature of the medium of the learning platform – the web browser – provides a number of intrinsic opportunities for this ‘sharing’ of the curriculum map and plan. Web pages do not need to be structured linearly, they can be hyperlinked together and users can determine their own paths. Content can be assembled and reassembled in different combinations to suit different needs.

More straightforwardly the way in which a course is presented online may reinforce its structure and internal plan so as to reinforce key learning across the course. The online space is less transient than materials handed out week by week. It allows pupils to return again and again to the same content (whether static files or collaborative activities) and to see them in relation to other aspects of the course. This has implications for course design – both in terms of planning and of design and use of resources. Trainee teachers should investigate the ways in which courses are planned and presented on learning platforms, and how they are used by different groups of pupils. The Becta requirements for this aspect include issues of accessibility, personalisation and navigation. These may provide a framework for evaluation.

Learner access and administration

As above, a learning platform may encompass elements of managed learning environments, which in turn may be linked to, or subsume, management information systems. These systems provide access to tracking data on usage of the electronic resources and on the progress of pupils through the school. Typical examples cited by Becta include attendance tracking, assessment monitoring, user account management and authentication, security and permissions, scheduling, timetabling, management of portfolios and usage data. Data entry to these systems may be through a teacher’s laptop, PDA or swipe card.

These functions rely on a number of underlying databases and ICT trainees could use this context to deepen their understanding of data. If possible, they should be given opportunities not only to set up resources for courses, but to manage user accounts for differentiated access. They should understand the design of different levels of permissions for different roles – pupil, teacher, administrator.

It is also essential that they make use of data from the systems to assist with their planning. This would provide evidence for QTS standard Q13 “Know how to use local and national statistical information to evaluate the effectiveness of their teaching, to monitor the progress of those they teach and to raise levels of attainment”.

Communication tools

As noted above the learning platform is a collaborative, interactive space. Content is created by all users and shared amongst them. There are a range of communication tools that may be provided – wikis, blogs, forums, chat etc - and these are considered in more detail in Software for collaboration on this website.

The learning platform may also incorporate a standard e-mail system. There are issues of privacy and workload to consider when pupils are in personal contact with teachers by e-mail. Trainees should be encouraged to discuss these issues, as reported by the BBC.

Users may be able to subscribe to the learning platform through an RSS or similar feed. This will allow them to be notified of updates and to receive regular materials, such as podcasts. Trainees should be aware of the technicalities involved in creating and receiving feeds for such services.

E-portfolio

The DfES e-strategy requires all pupils to have access to a digital repository. One way of providing this is in the form of an e-portfolio. Such a space provides opportunity for the storage, assessment and showcasing of pupil work. In some cases the e-portfolio may also facilitate transfer between schools and other sectors as it will itself be transferable. Trainees should discuss the ways in which ICT can help with the transfer process through an e-portfolio. They should also discuss the drawbacks, technical and social, in building up large folios of electronic work.

A new resource on e-portfolios and e-assessment is being developed for this website during 2008.

Reusable learning objects (RLOs)

Activities and resources can be bundled together to construct a ‘learning object’. This is a discrete entity in the learning platform’s digital repository. The concept of reusability of learning objects implies the detaching of the object from any assessments, feedback or individual user. It usually is coupled with concepts of interoperability whereby objects can be moved from one platform to another according to a standardised protocol, such as SCORM.

Hardware developments


This page discusses a number of pieces of hardware that trainee teachers may find in the classroom.
  • Interactive whiteboard (IWB)
  • Portable Devices
- Connectivity
- Laptops
- Tablet PCs
- Handheld devices
- Games consoles
- Voting systems
  • Other devices
As with all aspects of their school placements while training, there are opportunities for trainees to share experiences – hardware that is common in one school may be unknown in another. They should also draw on their pupils' experience as new hardware often finds its way into homes before it finds its way into schools. An example of this is mature market for handheld games machines in homes compared to the newly-emerging use of such devices in schools. Extending this, trainees might usefully consider the policies for use of devices brought into school. For example, is the mobile phone a tool for the bully, a distraction to learning or a multi-media information device?

Interactive whiteboard (IWB)

Interactive whiteboards provide opportunities for whole class interactive teaching and learning. By this is meant the interactivity that is gained by having a versatile resource that can be manipulated by any one pupil (or the teacher) and seen by the whole class. This includes control of presentations, rearrangement of items on screen e.g. to match and develop concepts, hyperlinking to multimedia objects, selection and restriction of screen areas to provide for focus.

IWBs are being used extensively in schools and colleges. It is essential that trainee teachers not only develop the skills and competence required to use IWBs successfully but they also understand how to interact with the boards and the pupils they teach. Among the various advantages of IWBs are:
  • allowing all members of the group to see resources, presentations and demonstrations clearly;
  • enabling smoother transitions between activities within a lesson, because diverse resources, such as word processor, video clips, web pages, images, etc, can be readily available and easily combined;
  • enabling complex processes to be demonstrated step by step to whole groups;
  • encouraging visualisation of difficult concepts and processes;
  • reinforcing communally constructed learning through interaction between students and the board, for example by providing a visual forum for compiling ideas on a topic.
The IWB itself is only part of a larger system. Trainees will need to understand how the software to control the board works. Most boards will inter-relate with other pieces of software, for example, to allow slides to be annotated by hand and resaved. IWB software usually provides additional functionality, such as reveal or magnify, and trainees should be encouraged to explore these tools with a view to evaluating their pedagogical impact.

An evaluation by Hall and Higgins (2005) of the use of IWBs in primary schools found that pupils were enthusiastic about their use and the ways in which they enlivened learning, but cited constraints due to access to the technology and associated technical and pedagogical problems. Hall and Higgins also reported on the tension between the use of the IWB and its drawing the teacher to the front of the class and the interactivity with pupils at their tables. Somekh et al's 2007 SWEEP report finds, however, that as there is an increase in interactivity the impact of the loss of group work is negligible.

The SWEEP report also highlights the multi-sensory nature of the use of IWBs, "The interactive whiteboard acts as a multi-modal portal, giving teachers the potential to use still images, moving images and sound," (p5).

Further sources of information are Becta's review of research and research from the Institute of Education. The last of these casts a critical eye on the pedagogical impact of IWBs and contrasts with the findings of the final evaluation of the four-year ICT Test Bed project which claims that “Effective use of presentation technologies led to greater interaction between teachers and learners” (p5).

Smith et al (2005) published a literature review in the Journal of Computer Assisted Learning. An updated review can be found in a 2007 special issue of Learning Media and Technology, which is entirely devoted to IWBs.

Portable Devices

The description ‘portable ICT device’ covers a wide range of machines with very varied functions. This section describes the use of some of these devices. In most case there is some form of connectivity between the device and a network or computer, in the case of peripherals.

Connectivity

Increasingly portable devices are connected via wireless local area networks using the 802.11 or Wi-Fi protocol. This is the method for connecting computers to a wireless hub or router and it is becoming more prevalent in other devices such as printers, projectors and cameras. The Becta survey on wireless found that
  • Fifty-seven per cent of primary schools and 34 per cent of secondary schools had over 75 per cent wireless coverage of their school buildings.
  • One per cent of primary schools and 22 per cent of secondary schools allow personally owned pupil devices onto the wireless network.
  • Twenty six per cent of primary schools and 42 per cent of secondary schools allow personally owned teacher devices onto the wireless network.
Trainee teachers should be encouraged to discuss the findings of this survey and reflect on their own circumstances in schools. The survey found that the most popular reason for implementing wireless was to support the use of ICT in classrooms other than ICT suites. This will have a profound effect on the way in which ICT is perceived by teachers and pupils and on the style of learning and teaching that can be adopted.

There have been numerous concerns about the safety of wireless networks, due to the increase in radio frequency (RF) electro-magnetic radiation. Becta’s advice is to follow the guidance of the Health Protection Agency and states that "There is no consistent evidence of health effects from RF exposures below guideline levels and therefore no reason why schools and others should not use wi-fi equipment."

Other methods of connectivity include Bluetooth, which is often used in conjunction with a USB dongle to allow for a device such as a wireless mouse to connect to a computer, and infra red.

Laptops

Schools are increasingly using laptops for teachers and pupils. Research in Scotland by Simpson and Payne reported
  • changed approaches in classroom management and organisation
  • increased use of ICT for administrative purposes
  • taking laptops home for homework and extension of work.
  • pupils becoming focused, motivated and confident users of ICT together with having the ability to work as independent learners
Set against these benefits trainees will need to consider the classroom management issues in using laptop computers (see also Managing the wireless classroom in the Behaviour Management resource on this website). Often these will be stored away and need to be started up at the start of a lesson. Considerations include movement around the classroom by pupils fetching computers, trailing mains leads and the need to ensure that the laptops are kept charged. Trolley or rack systems are available to charge class sets of laptops.

A further issue is the taking up of table space by the introduction of computers. The use of newer ultra-portable notebooks provides a smaller ‘footprint’ and also allows pupils to carry the devices around more easily should they need to.

Tablet PCs

Tablet PCs provide additional functionality to that of a traditional laptop and this opens up new possibilities for using them with trainee teachers and pupils. The screen of the computer twists flat and can be used as a touch sensitive writing tablet. This allows for non-keyed entry, which provides for more flexibility.

Wireless tablet PCs or laptops can be used in conjunction with wireless projectors to allow pupil screens to be displayed at the front of the class. This provides opportunities for teachers to work at a table with the pupils while demonstrating to the whole class. In doing so, similar pedagogical benefits to an IWB are reported but with the additional advantage of not placing the teacher at the front of the class. This gives the pupils more ownership of their learning.

Case studies of their use can be used with trainees. Two such studies are of Djanogly City Academy and Cornwallis School.

Handheld devices

While laptops and tablets are fully functioning PCs, there is also increasing use of smaller, cheaper devices such as palmtops and PDAs. These have the advantage of easier storage and mobility. Trainees should explore how such devices are being used in their placement schools and colleges. They should be made aware of the potential opportunities when learning takes place out of traditional classroom settings. These include on the sports field, on field trips and in settings such as museums. Examples of use can be found in Perry’s report for Becta and in the case study of Warren School’s collaboration with a local museum. PDAs are also being used increasingly with e-books – both those obtained from publishers and those created by pupils.

Mobile phones are increasingly becoming multi-functional and can carry out many of the tasks that a PDA can. They have the added advantage of being very readily available to pupils. Nevertheless, as referred to earlier, their use in schools is increasingly controversial - see, for example this BBC report, a blog post by a teacher and Becta's advice.

Other handheld devices include calculators and data logging devices, which have specialist application in mathematics and science. Trainees should explore how the use of these can contribute to the National Curriculum in those subjects and in ICT.

Games consoles

Pupils increasingly have access to games consoles at home. Some schools are now making use of these devices to complement their more traditional ICT hardware. As reported in Computing, such devices can increasingly act as PDAs as well as games consoles and have the advantage of high quality video processors.

Voting systems

Class sets of voting systems allow pupils to respond to questions and for charts of their responses to be displayed. Typically there will be one transceiver plugged into a USB port, one teacher’s keypad and a number of pupil keypads. Some systems allow for free text entry as well as numeric responses.

A Becta case study into their use at two schools found that voting sytems
  • enable teachers to quickly check pupils' understanding
  • enable teachers to gauge pupils’ opinions
  • enable all pupils to actively participate in lessons
  • reduce the amount of marking needed
  • provide for easy tracking of pupils' progress

Other devices

Trainees should be encouraged to share their experiences of other devices used in classrooms. These may include wireless keyboards and mice, clickers and pointers, which allow pupils and teachers to interact with displays without coming to the front of the class.

They also include still and moving digital video cameras and audio recording equipment and headphones. The use of ‘green’ or ‘blue’ screens for overlaying videos is becoming more prevalent.

Visualisers are being found in a range of settings and 3D printers are being introduced into design and technology lessons.

MP3 and MP4 players are being used by pupils for music and video, but they can also be used to download educational content or subscribe to podcasts.

Finally, there is a range of devices associated with identity management such as fingerprint scanners, swipe cards and cashless canteen systems. Trainees should be encouraged to discuss the social implications of these.

Multimedia


Multimedia includes the use, and combination of, text, audio, still images, animation, video, and interactive content, such as hyperlinks. The use of ICT tools allows trainees, pupils and tutors to create resources and products in a range of media. Such activities provide especially well for addressing creativity. They also allow for support of multi-sensory learning and trainees should be encouraged to explore their use in the context of meeting special needs and differentiation. These help address the requirements of standards Q19, Q25a and Q25d in particular. In addition, with all of the tools there are opportunities for developing group and team work and for problem solving.

A number of tools are considered on this page
  • Digital Video
  • Multimedia books
  • Video conferencing
  • Podcasts
  • Animation
For each of the tools covered on this page, trainees could review the benefits and approaches reported and reflect on how they might include the use of the tools in their school settings. Tutors could provide activities to develop the use of the tools and the understanding of their particular affordances and the influence they have on pedagogy.

Digital Video

Digital video provides a medium whereby pupils, and teachers, can create audio and video productions and then manipulate them using ICT. Research summarised by Becta listed advantages for pupils and teachers. These included increased motivation, literacy skills, collaboration, creativity and self-expression and community-based learning.

The use of green or blue screen technology allows for videos to be mixed with other digital presentations. An example of their use is provided by Coseley School.

Learning and Teaching Scotland have a range of advice and case studies on the use of digital images and video. They summarise the benefits of using digital video as
  • The use of video can help improve narrative, literacy and storytelling skills.
  • Creativity skills are developed.
  • ICT skills in editing techniques are covered.
  • Pupils are actively engaged in the activity.
  • Pupils are encouraged to collaborate with each other.
  • Movie-making can be a highly motivational exercise.

Loveless, Burton and Turvey (2006) report in Thinking Skills and Creativity on a project in which trainee teachers investigate their classroom practice in the use of ICT to promote creativity through the making of digital video movies, and to reflect upon the development of their pedagogy with ICT in primary classrooms.

Multimedia books

Multimedia, or talking, books may be created by or for pupils. These combine traditional text and images in slide show format with audio, and less commonly video. Examples of their use are in supporting young readers, pupils with English as an additional language (EAL) or with particular special educational needs.

The Portsmouth EMAS service provide a website of case studies of the production of such ‘books’ int he context of English as an dddiitonal language.

The National Strategy 's primary framework for literacy lists a range of use for ICT in supporting literacy activities. These include the creation of multimedia books. Examples of activites listed include KS2 pupils producing talking picture books intended for children in a reception class and the creation of multimedia presentations of pupils’ or other writers’ poems.

Video conferencing

Using video conferencing (VC) allows trainee teachers to be placed in an environment where they can support or be supported remotely. VC enables trainees and tutors in different locations to talk to and see each other. VC sessions may also allow digital files to be exchanged or shared and enables trainees, or trainees and tutors, to work together remotely on a single file. The Open University have developed a Flash-based system for VC so that the costs are very low – participants requiring only a simple webcam.

As reported in The Guardian, Nottingham University and the TDA have researched the use of VC for observing trainees on placement. This extends the more common use of video for assessment of teaching either by the trainees themselves, their peers or their tutors.

The Global Leap website provides further examples of the use of VC in education.

Video conferencing also provides opportunities for pupils to learn with others remote from the classroom. These may include pupils in other schools, or teachers or other experts in other locations. Trainees could experiment with teaching remotely using this technology, maybe to their peers in the first instance. Such use of remote experts can also be supported by other technologies, such as a discussion forum.

Practical guides that focus of the technical and pedagogical issues of using VC are available from a Becta-funded project carried out by WMnet.

Podcasts

A podcast is an audio or video file that is uploaded to a server and that can be subscribed to using suitable software. It can then be transferred to an MP3 or MP4 player (the latter supporting video). Schools can make use of this for providing short pieces of ‘content’. Further, pupils can be engaged in the creation of their own podcasts.

Technical issues in the use of podcasts by training providers, or in schools, are associated with the need to upload the files and then subscribe to them. This may involve ftp access to, and RSS feed from, a server. Trainees should understand these technical issues. A partial solution is to share audio files without automatic subscription and trainees should be able to explain the differences between the two methods.

A case study of the collaborative use of podcasts is available from Oakington and St Mary Magdalene Schools. A larger project of a school radio station, incorporating podcasts, is available from The Downs School.

Animation

Animations may be created in two dimensions, using Flash or other graphics software, or in three dimensions using cameras and ‘stop-frame’ software. The Digital Creativity website has a series of case studies of its use with pupils and teachers.

The FIlm Education website gives some guidance on the use of animations with pupils. They stress that 'Animation' is a performing art rather than a graphic art and has much in common with drama. This highlights the need for team work.

Software for collaboration


ICT provides opportunities of collaboration in the creation and sharing of resources and also the joint construction of knowledge and understanding through communication tools. This page discusses a range of these tools
  • Blogs
  • Wikis
  • Discussion forums
  • Social networking
  • Web-based applications
Research evidence about the impact of ICT in ITT includes the following key benefits
  • improved communication between staff and students
  • enhanced peer support
  • extended links between campus and classroom.
  • use of ICT to provide communication links between students on teaching practice
This page discusses the use of collaboration and communication tools that trainees may find their pupils using or that they may wish to introduce into their classrooms.

Trainees should be made aware of the issues surrounding the use of these tools. These include
  • Access issues - do the school, local authority or RBC networks allow access?
  • Can filters and ports be reconfigured to allow access, or does this contravene current policies?
  • What are the acceptable use conditions and is there a policy in place?
  • What are the professional ethical issues involved in trainee-trainee interaction - for example in making coments about placement schools, mentors and tutors?
  • What are the ethical issues involved in contact with pupils via these tools?
  • What are the e-safety implications of pupils posting online?
A set of resources have been produced by Childnet, with input from ITTE, on e-safety. These include guidelines for trainee teachers.

Blogs

Originally conceived of as a personal online diary the weblog allows the owner to post items that are stamped by the time of their creation and with keywords chosen by the author. In this sense it is a tool to aid self-reflection or evaluation

Items or posts are usually, but always, quite short and may be linked to other items through hyperlinks, keywords or shared taxonomies or bookmarks such as Digg, Reddit or Delicious.

Each item in the blog can usually be commented upon and this opens up the possibility of discussion around a topic. This is one way in which collaboration can take place. One pupil posts and the others comment. Another way is to have multiple owners/authors of a blog.

Blogs are implicitly public as they are posted on the web. Teachers, and pupils, can usually restrict access through a range of means – hiding the blog from search engines, named access only, passwords, etc. Trainees should consider a range of blogging environments and evaluate them for privacy and safety. Becta provide a set of guidelines on their safe and acceptable use.

Files may be attached to blog posts and so they provide a simple example of a personal e-portfolio.

A case study of their use is provided by O’Hear on Teachers TV.

Wikis

A wiki is a collaboratively created and edited website consisting of a set of pages. Trainees, and pupils, will be familiar with Wikipedia but may not have used it for editing and creation of new items. Similarly they may not have created their own wiki from scratch. One particular use with trainees is the co-creation of resources and plans for team teaching or for sharing resources across distance when on placement. In this sense they form a collaborative e-portfolio.

There are similar privacy and safety considerations with wikis as with blogs. Additionally the ability to edit any one else’s work requires quite clear codes of conduct to be negotiated.

In seeing that anyone can edit anything, trainees should also be able to discuss the reliability of Wikipedia and other such resources.

Research into the use of wikis has been carried out by Grant for Futurelab. This highlights, in particular the collaboration and joint construction of new content by pupils.

Discussion forums

While blogs, and wikis, allow for comments to be posted onto items they are not intrinsically designed for discussions. Trainees should consider the features available in the tools that they use and make informed choices as their use. They should also review the tools available on the VLE in their schools and providers.

Boulton and Bradshaw provide a case study of the use of a range of communication tools with PGCE trainee teachers.

Download Word document: Use of weblogs and other.doc (49K)

Social networking

Trainees may use sites such as MySpace, Bebo and Facebook for their own social interactions. Their pupils will almost certainly do the same. There has also been some use of such spaces to support learning.

These sites provide ample opportunity to discuss issues of e-safety - see Childnet resources and the CEOP website. Further they are examples of large data sets of users with associated attributes. This may provide a fruitful context for the study of data in the new ICT National Curriculum.

Futurelab identify two converging trends in their ‘Opening Education’ report. They identify a change in the educational arena as to the purpose of education and the role of teachers and learners. This, they say, is matched by the change in the development of technologies that allows for learning and collaborating together.

When considering social networking sites, trainees should be made aware of the issues that arise through extended networks, as identified in this TES article, where teachers were putting unsuitable material into their online space.

Web-based applications

Further collaboration opportunities are provided by web-based word processors, spreadsheets etc.

Futurelab's Why Don't You... project is largely concerned with how such tools might be influencing education. Little research has yet (2008) been done on the use of such tools. Trainees should be encouraged to explore their use and how they might provide opportunities for collaboration, learning beyond the classroom and portability of electronic artefacts.

They should also be made aware of the copyright and intellectual property right issues with storing work online. Usually the ownership resides with the provider of the tool, not of the author.
